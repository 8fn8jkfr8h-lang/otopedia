Allergic Fungal Rhinosinusitis

"AFRS / Eosinophilic Fungal Rhinosinusitis"

Inflammatory Disease

ICD-10: J32.9 / J33.8

Faculty Note: AFRS is a non-invasive, fungus-mediated inflammatory pansinusitis. Characterized by "allergic mucin" and a robust Th2 immune response. It often causes silent sinus expansion and bony erosion. Success requires thorough surgical debridement followed by aggressive long-term medical therapy.

1 Epidemiology & Demographics

Key Characteristics

  • Incidence: 5–10% of all chronic rhinosinusitis cases requiring surgery.
  • Climate: Strong preference for warm, humid regions (Southern US, India, SE Asia).
  • Gender: Slight male predominance (~60%).
  • Age: Primarily adolescents and young adults (Median: 20–30 years).

Primary Risk Factors

Atopy / AR

Asthma

Humid Climate

Age Distribution at Presentation

Peak incidence occurs in the 2nd and 3rd decades of life.

2 Pathogenesis & Etiology

Causative Fungi

Bipolaris spicifera Curvularia Aspergillus Alternaria

Common environmental molds driving a dysregulated hypersensitivity response.

Immunological Markers

  • Total Serum IgE >1000 IU/mL
  • Peripheral Eosinophilia Common
  • Specific Fungal IgE Positive (Type I)
  • Cytokine Profile Th2 (IL-4, IL-5)

The "Allergic Mucin" Profile

Gross Appearance

Tenacious, viscid, and typically described as having a "peanut butter" or "machine oil" consistency.

Microscopy

Eosinophil-rich debris with Charcot-Leyden crystals and scattered non-invasive fungal hyphae.

Tissue Effect

Accumulation causes pressure-induced bone remodeling and thinning, potentially leading to expansion into the orbit.

3 Diagnostic Workup

Bent & Kuhn Diagnostic Criteria (1994)

1. Hypersensitivity

Type I (Skin/RAST)

2. Nasal Polyps

Diffuse Polyposis

3. CT Findings

Hyperattenuation

4. Eosinophilic Mucin

Non-invasive

5. Fungal Stain

GMS / Culture (+)

CT (Non-Contrast)

Look for the "Double Density" sign: hyperattenuating mucin centers surrounded by lower attenuation inflammatory mucosa. Sinus expansion and bone thinning are common.

MRI (Contrast)

T2-weighted images show a signal void (dark appearance) in areas of dense fungal mucin. Superior for evaluating orbital or intracranial extension.

Histopathology

GMS or PAS stain is essential to visualize fungal hyphae within the mucin. Mucin should show degranulating eosinophils and Charcot-Leyden crystals.

4 Staging Systems

Kupferberg (Endoscopic) is used for clinical surveillance. Bent & Kuhn (CT) for anatomical extent.

5 Management Algorithm

Standard Treatment Protocol

1
Pre-operative Steroids

Short course of oral Prednisone to reduce polyp mass and vascularity.

2
FESS (Complete Clearance)

Wide drainage and meticulous removal of all allergic mucin and fungal debris.

3
Post-operative Irrigation

Budesonide or Fluticasone rinses are the mainstay of long-term maintenance.

4
Surveillance (Total IgE)

Monitor IgE levels every 3-6 months as a barometer for recurrence.

Clinical Pearl

"Total Serum IgE is the 'ESR' of AFRS." A rising IgE level post-operatively often precedes clinical recurrence of polyps and mucin. Establish a baseline post-clearance IgE level once the patient is stable.

Trend Watch

Biologics: Monoclonal antibodies targeting the Th2 pathway (e.g., Dupilumab) are showing great promise for refractory AFRS cases, potentially reducing the need for repeat surgeries.

Warning

Avoid oral antifungals as primary therapy; their efficacy is unproven and potential for side effects (liver toxicity) is high. Focus remains on local control and steroids.

6 Prognosis & Recurrence

Recurrence Rate by Adherence

Long-term Outlook

Recurrence is common (30-60%) due to the chronic nature of the hypersensitivity response. However, permanent complications are rare with proper management.
