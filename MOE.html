Malignant Otitis Externa

"Necrotizing Otitis Externa / Skull Base Osteomyelitis"

Critical Condition

ICD-10: H60.20

Faculty Note: MOE is an invasive, potentially lethal infection of the external auditory canal that extends into the skull base. Historically "malignant" due to its aggressive spread, it primarily affects elderly diabetics. Success hinges on early recognition, prolonged antipseudomonal therapy, and aggressive management of comorbidities.

1 Epidemiology & Demographics

Key Patient Profile

  • Mean Age: 70.3 Years (Range 18-99). Risk increases >60.
  • Gender: Strong male predominance (1.45:1 to 2:1 ratio).
  • Incidence: 1142% rise in England (2002-2017).
  • Key Comorbidities: Diabetes (57-100%), Hypertension (51%).

Predisposing Factors

Uncontrolled DM
Smoking History
Microangiopathy
Water Exposure

Incidence Trend (England)

Dramatic rise in cases over 15 years due to aging and DM prevalence.

2 Etiology & Pathogenesis

MOE is driven by a convergence of host vulnerability (diabetes/immunosuppression) and pathogen virulence. The infection spreads along fascial planes and through the Haversian system of bone.

Pseudomonas aeruginosa

Non-commensal organism in the healthy ear. Uses proteolytic enzymes (elastase, alkaline protease) and Exotoxin A to degrade host tissues and evade the immune response. Biofilm formation provides a physical barrier against antibiotics.

Mechanisms of Bone Invasion

Tissue Sequence

Cellulitis → Chondritis → Periostitis → Osteitis → Osteomyelitis.

Leaping Focus

Bacteria invade small vascular channels (Haversian system), causing localized vasculitis.

Anatomical Shortcuts

Spread through Fissures of Santorini to parotid, TMJ, and parapharyngeal space.

3 Clinical Presentation

The Classic Clinical Tetrad

Exasperating Otalgia

Deep, severe, worse at night.

Persistent Otorrhea

Foul-smelling, purulent discharge.

Granulation Tissue

Found at EAC floor/junction.

Cranial Neuropathy

Indicates invasive progression.

Red Flags & Exam Findings

  • Mastoid Tenderness: Intense pain on palpation of the mastoid tip.
  • TMJ Pain: Indicates anterior spread through Fissures of Santorini.
  • EAC Stenosis: Severe edema often occludes the canal.
  • TM Integrity: Usually intact; perforation is a late finding.

Neurological Manifestations (9-27%)

CN VII (Facial) 91% of neuropathies
CN IX, X, XI (Jugular Foramen) Dysphagia, Hoarseness
CN XII (Hypoglossal) Tongue Deviation
CN VI, V, III (Petrous Apex) Terminal Stage

4 Diagnostic Workup

Laboratory & Cultures

Baseline inflammatory markers and glucose control are essential for monitoring.

  • ESR/CRP: Markedly elevated; key for monitoring treatment response.
  • HbA1c: Guides aggressive diabetes management.
  • Cultures: Ear swab (pre-drops) is mandatory.
GOLD STANDARD

MRI & CT Imaging

Combines bone detail with superior soft tissue resolution.

  • MRI (T1/T2/Gad): Best for marrow edema and dural involvement.
  • CT (Bone): Shows cortical erosion and demineralization.

Nuclear Medicine

Functional imaging remains critical in the MOE diagnostic algorithm.

  • Technetium-99m: High sensitivity for bone turnover; remains positive.
  • Gallium-67: Binds to leukocytes; returns to normal with resolution.
Clinical Pearl: Biopsy is Mandatory

Any granulation tissue in the EAC floor must be biopsied to rule out squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), which can mimic the presentation of MOE perfectly.

Levenson's Diagnostic Criteria (1991)

Obligatory (Major) Criteria
  • Refractory otitis externa (>1 week)
  • Severe nocturnal otalgia
  • Purulent otorrhea
  • Granulation tissue in the EAC
  • Positive Tc-99m bone scan
Minor (Occasional) Criteria
  • Diabetes mellitus / Advanced age
  • Cranial nerve palsy
  • Positive CT findings (bone erosion)
  • Isolation of P. aeruginosa or S. aureus

5 Staging & Classification

Gleeson is common for clinical practice. Clival staging predicts clival involvement severity.

6 Management Algorithm

1. Initial Stabilization

Meticulous glycemic control (Insulin preferred) and analgesia.

2. Systemic Antibiotics

Ciprofloxacin (Oral) or Ceftazidime (IV) for 6 weeks - 6 months.

3. Refractory / Atypical

Voriconazole for fungal; Meropenem for resistant Pseudomonas.

Surgery & Adjuncts

  • Biopsy/Debridement: Mandatory to rule out malignancy and improve hygiene.
  • Abscess Drainage: For localized parapharyngeal or mastoid collections.
  • HBOT: 100% Oxygen at >1 ATM for refractory cases (40-60 sessions).
Termination Criteria: Treatment completion is based on clinical resolution, normalization of ESR/CRP, and a negative Gallium-67 scan. Antibiotics are often continued for 1 week post-normalization.
Critical Alert: Facial Nerve Palsy

New-onset facial nerve palsy in an elderly diabetic with otitis externa is MOE until proven otherwise. It indicates infection has reached the stylomastoid foramen.

Trend Watch: PET/MRI

Emerging research suggests PET/MRI is superior for distinguishing residual inflammation from active bone infection, potentially reducing antibiotic durations.

7 Prognosis & Outcomes

Estimated Survival Probability

Overall 5-year survival remains around 51% due to comorbidities and disease severity.

18% Global Mortality
40% Adverse Outcome

Predictors of Mortality

  • Multiple CN Palsies 80% Risk
  • Fungal Etiology High Risk
  • Age > 70 Years Adverse
