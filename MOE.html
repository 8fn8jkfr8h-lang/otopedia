Malignant Otitis Externa

"Skull Base Osteomyelitis"

Diabetic Complication

Pseudomonas aeruginosa

Faculty Note: MOE is a severe, potentially fatal infection of the external auditory canal and skull base, predominantly affecting diabetics. Early recognition and aggressive treatment are crucial to prevent progression to cranial nerve palsies and intracranial complications.

1 Epidemiology & Demographics

Key Characteristics

  • Incidence: 1-5 per 100,000 population, higher in diabetics.
  • Age: Predominantly affects elderly (>60 years).
  • Gender: Slight male predominance.
  • Risk Factors: Diabetes mellitus (90-100% of cases), immunocompromised states.

Clinical Presentation

Severe Otalgia

Otorrhea

Conductive Hearing Loss

Microbiology Distribution

Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the predominant pathogen.

2 Pathophysiology

The Pathological Cascade

MOE is not merely an external ear infection; it is a progressive osteomyelitis of the temporal bone. It exploits the microangiopathy of diabetes and the natural anatomical weaknesses of the external auditory canal (EAC).

Step 1

EAC Acidification Failure

Alkaline cerumen in diabetics promotes Pseudomonas growth.

Step 2

Soft Tissue Invasion

Passage through Fissures of Santorini or Tympanomastoid suture.

Step 3

Bone Destruction

Haversian system invasion leads to extensive skull base necrosis.

Organism Profile

Pseudomonas aeruginosa dominates in MOE.

Diagnostic Workup

  • Imaging: CT temporal bone for bony erosion; MRI for soft tissue extent; Tc-99m bone scan for osteomyelitis confirmation.
  • Laboratory: ESR and CRP for monitoring; blood glucose and HbA1c; culture from EAC drainage.
  • Biopsy: Granulation tissue biopsy to rule out malignancy (e.g., squamous cell carcinoma).
  • Other: Audiometry for hearing loss; cranial nerve examination for palsies.

4 Staging Systems

C Chandler Staging System

Stage I: EAC & Soft Tissue only.
Stage II: Bone involvement (Osteitis).
Stage III: Extension to Parotid, Mastoid, or Infratemporal Fossa.
Stage IV: Cranial Nerve palsy or Intracranial extension.

B Benecke Classification

Grade 1: Soft tissue infection (EAC/Parotid).
Grade 2: Skull base osteomyelitis (Limited).
Grade 3: Skull base osteomyelitis (Extensive + CN Palsy).
Grade 4: Intracranial complications (Meningitis/Abscess).

5 Management Algorithm

1. Antimicrobial Strategy

Primary Monotherapy

**Ciprofloxacin** (750mg PO BID or 400mg IV TID). High bone penetration.

Resistance / Severe Cases

**Ceftazidime** (2g IV q8h) + Aminoglycoside or Piperacillin-Tazobactam.

Fungal MOE (Aspergillus)

Voriconazole or Amphotericin B if Pseudomonas culture is negative.

2. Metabolic & HBOT

Glycemic Optimization

HbA1c reduction. Strict monitoring. MOE will NOT resolve in uncontrolled hyperglycemia.

Aural Toilet

Frequent, meticulous debridement of necrotic debris in clinic.

Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBOT)

Indicated for refractory cases (Stage III/IV). Increases phagocytic activity of PMNs in hypoxic bone.

3. Surgical Indications

Surgery is generally avoided (risk of exposing more bone to infection) except for:

6 Prognosis & Survival

Survival & Clinical Variables

*Prognosis significantly worsens with each additional cranial nerve involved.

Monitoring Resolution

ESR & CRP

Weekly tracking. Failure of ESR to fall suggests treatment resistance or non-compliance.

Gallium-67 vs. Indium-111

Gallium scans bind to iron-binding proteins in leukocytes. When the Gallium scan normalizes, it indicates biological resolution. Treatment should continue for 1 week post-normalization.

Recurrence Rate

Approximately 15-20% recurrence if treatment duration is < 6 weeks.

80-90% Localized Disease (5-yr)
50-70% Advanced Disease (5-yr)
20-40% Cranial Nerve Palsy (5-yr)
< 10% Intracranial Extension (5-yr)
Note: Mortality is high without treatment (up to 50% in diabetics). Early diagnosis and aggressive therapy are critical.
