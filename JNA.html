Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma

"JNA / Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma"

Highly Vascular Tumor

ICD-10: D10.6

Faculty Note: JNA is a histologically benign but clinically aggressive, highly vascular tumor occurring almost exclusively in adolescent males. Characterized by extreme vascularity and a tendency for local invasion. CRITICAL: Never biopsy a suspected JNA in an office setting.

1 Epidemiology & Demographics

Key Statistics

  • Incidence: 0.05% - 0.5% of all head and neck tumors.
  • Population Rate: 1 in 150,000 to 1 in 600,000 individuals.
  • Gender: Nearly 100% Male predominance.
  • Median Age: 14–15 years (Range: 9–25).

Risk Factors & Associations

Gardner Syndrome / FAP

25-fold increased risk in patients with Familial Adenomatous Polyposis.

Geographic Variation

Higher incidence in India, Egypt, and Southeast Asia.

Age Distribution at Presentation

Peak incidence occurs during adolescent puberty.

2 Pathogenesis & Molecular Profile

Hormonal Influence

Growth is fueled by testosterone. High expression of Androgen Receptors (AR). Estrogen/Progesterone receptors usually absent.

Genetic Markers

Frequent mutations in Wnt/β-catenin pathway. Loss of chromosomes 8, 17, Y; Gain of X, 4, 6, 8.

Growth Factors

High expression of VEGF and TGF-β contributes to intense neovascularization and tumor growth.

Histopathological Characteristics

Vascular Component

Rich network of endothelium-lined channels. Crucial: These vessels lack a muscular coat (tunica media), explaining the inability to vasoconstrict and the risk of massive hemorrhage.

Stromal Component

Dense fibrous stroma with spindle-shaped or stellate fibroblasts. Non-encapsulated growth allows for finger-like projections into adjacent spaces.

3 Clinical Presentation

The Classic Diagnostic Triad

Unilateral Obstruction

Present in 80-90% of cases. Progressive and painless.

Recurrent Epistaxis

Profuse, painless, and typically unilateral (45-60%).

Nasopharyngeal Mass

Visible on endoscopy as a lobulated, rubbery, reddish mass.

Symptom Prevalence

Headache (Sinus Obstruction) 25%
Facial Swelling (ITF Involvement) 10-15%
Proptosis (Orbital Extension) 10-15%
Hyponasal Speech 5-10%
Clinical Pearl: Otoscopy

Always perform otoscopy. JNA often presents with unilateral serous otitis media due to Eustachian tube dysfunction from mass effect.

Inspection Tip

Look for cheek fullness (frog-face deformity) in advanced stages, indicating massive expansion into the infratemporal fossa.

4 Diagnostic Workup

Contrast-Enhanced CT

Essential for evaluating bony erosion and the skull base.

HOLMAN-MILLER SIGN: Anterior bowing of the posterior maxillary sinus wall.
GOLD STANDARD

MRI with Gadolinium

Superior for soft tissue and intracranial extension evaluation.

"Salt and pepper" appearance (flow voids) on T2-weighted images.

Biopsy (Warning)

Office biopsy is strictly contraindicated.

RISK OF MASSIVE HEMORRHAGE. Defer until definitive surgery.

Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA)

Critical for mapping the blood supply and performing Preoperative Embolization.

  • Primary supply: Internal Maxillary Artery (IMA).
  • Potential supply: Ascending pharyngeal or branches of ICA.

Embolization Goal

60 - 80%

Reduction in Blood Loss

5 Staging & Classification

Fisch and Radkowski are the primary systems. Radkowski is preferred for surgical planning.

6 Management Algorithm

1. Preoperative Prep

DSA + Embolization within 24–48h of surgery to minimize blood loss.

2. Primary Surgery (EEA)

Endoscopic Endonasal Approach is the gold standard for most Fisch I-III tumors.

3. External Approaches

Weber-Fergusson or Craniofacial resection for massive intracranial extension.

Second-Line / Adjuncts

  • Radiotherapy: (30-45 Gy) for inoperable, residual, or recurrent disease.
  • Medical Therapy: Hormonal blockers (e.g., Flutamide) show mixed results.
  • Surveillance: MRI every 6 months for 2 years, then annually for 5 years.
Critical Warning: Hemorrhage

Intraoperative blood loss can exceed 2-3 Liters without proper embolization. Always have blood products available.

Trend Watch: Endoscopic First

Modern endoscopic techniques have significantly reduced morbidity, even for tumors with moderate intracranial extension.

7 Prognosis & Outcomes

Recurrence Rate by Stage (Radkowski)

Advanced stages involving the skull base significantly increase recurrence risk.

~100% Overall Survival
10-20% Avg Recurrence

Prognostic Factors

Favorable

  • Total Resection
  • Successful Embolization
  • Age > 15 Years

Adverse

  • Intracranial Extension
  • ICA Involvement
  • Age < 15 Years
Surveillance Protocol: Follow-up MRI every 6 months for 2 years, then annually for at least 5 years. Most recurrences occur within 12–18 months.
